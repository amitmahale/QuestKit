# QuestKit PRD

## 1. Product Summary

**Product name:** QuestKit  
**Tagline:** Turn curiosity into play.

QuestKit is a family learning engine that converts books, science topics, school units, and random curiosities into interactive experiences such as quizzes, scavenger hunts, car-ride trivia, mini escape-room challenges, and short adaptive games.

It is designed for families who want kids to learn more often, with less friction, and without turning every moment into formal homework. The product combines personalization, voice-friendly interaction, and light memory techniques like spaced repetition, but hides the mechanics behind fun, variety, and ritual.

The core wedge is **family curiosity and repeatable learning rituals**, not school compliance and not answer-generation for homework.

---

## 2. Problem

Parents want their kids to learn beyond school, but most options fail in one of four ways:

1. They feel like homework, so kids resist.
2. They are chaotic or overstimulating, so parents avoid using them regularly.
3. They are generic, so they do not fit the child’s age, reading level, interests, or current school topics.
4. They solve for answers, not learning, which weakens trust and long-term engagement.

Families also have many “dead spaces” that could become learning moments: car rides, dinner table conversations, bedtime reading, waiting rooms, weekend boredom, and transitions between activities. Existing products rarely turn those moments into structured but light learning rituals.

---

## 3. Vision

Build the default family layer for playful learning.

QuestKit should become the thing a parent opens when they want to say:

* “Make this chapter fun.”
* “Turn this science topic into a game.”
* “Give us five car questions on this.”
* “Make a scavenger hunt for my 10-year-old.”
* “Help my kid remember this next week without it feeling like review.”

Long term, QuestKit should feel less like an app and more like a household engine that transforms any content into the right learning mode for the moment.

---

## 4. Goals

### Primary goals

* Increase the frequency of family learning interactions.
* Make learning feel playful, short, and repeatable.
* Personalize content by age, reading level, topic familiarity, and mode.
* Create strong parent trust through quality, control, and low-chaos UX.
* Create durable retention through rituals, saved topics, and memory loops.

### Secondary goals

* Support independent kid use through voice-first or low-friction play.
* Help parents reuse books, school topics, and random curiosities as learning fuel.
* Build a content flywheel where every interaction improves future adaptation.

### Non-goals for MVP

* Full homework solver.
* General answer engine for worksheets.
* Live tutoring marketplace.
* Full curriculum replacement.
* Social network for kids.
* Open-ended child chat without constraints.

## 7. Core Product Principles

1. **Fun first, substance underneath.**  
   The product should feel playful on the surface while quietly delivering learning depth.

2. **Parent-trusted, kid-loved.**  
   The experience must satisfy both sides. If it is only fun, parents churn. If it is only educational, kids churn.

3. **Low setup, high variation.**  
   A parent should be able to turn a topic into a playable format almost instantly.

4. **Adaptive without being obvious.**  
   Difficulty adjustment and spaced repetition should feel natural, not clinical.

5. **Modes for moments.**  
   The same topic should be transformable for different contexts: solo, family, car, bedtime, voice, movement, reading support.

6. **Curiosity over compliance.**  
   The product should encourage exploration and recall, not answer dependency.

## 9. Key Use Cases

### Use case 1: Turn a chapter into a game

A parent uploads or pastes a chapter title / text excerpt. QuestKit returns:

* a 5-minute quiz
* a story challenge
* a scavenger hunt around the house
* a mini mystery game based on the chapter

### Use case 2: Car ride trivia

A parent says, “Give me 10 car questions about the solar system for a 9-year-old.” QuestKit delivers voice-friendly short questions with hints and scorekeeping.

### Use case 3: Science topic reinforcement

A child is learning ecosystems in school. QuestKit generates adaptive quiz rounds, challenge cards, and follow-up questions over the next week.

### Use case 4: Curiosity-to-play

A child asks, “Why do sharks not drown?” Parent opens QuestKit, which turns the question into a mini learning adventure with 3 facts, 5 questions, and a challenge.

### Use case 5: Family challenge night

QuestKit creates a 15-minute mini escape-room challenge from a chosen theme like Ancient Egypt, fractions, or Harry Potter-style logic.

## 10. MVP Scope

### MVP thesis

The MVP should prove one thing: **families will repeatedly return to turn content into short playable learning moments.**

### MVP target audience

Parents with children ages 7–12.

This age band is broad enough for meaningful personalization but narrow enough to keep UX and content quality manageable.

### MVP core inputs

* typed topic
* pasted short text or chapter excerpt
* selected category from library (space, animals, history, reading comprehension, science)

### MVP core outputs

* Quick Quiz
* Car Trivia
* Scavenger Hunt
* Mini Challenge
* Chapter-to-Game

### MVP must-have features

#### A. Onboarding and profile setup

* child age
* reading comfort level
* favorite topics
* preferred play styles
* family mode defaults (calm, balanced, energetic)
* content safety guardrails

#### B. Content generation flow

Input: topic, text, or chapter  
Settings:

* mode
* duration
* difficulty
* solo vs family
* voice or text

Output:

* structured playable activity
* hints
* answer key / learning notes for parent
* retry or remix button

#### C. Parent mode

* preview activity before launching
* edit tone, length, and difficulty
* choose “more educational” vs “more playful”
* suppress chaos: fewer animations, calmer pacing, less randomness
* simple dashboard with recent topics, saved activities, and child progress themes

#### D. Kid mode

* one-tap start
* big buttons / simple voice prompts
* encouragement language
* adaptive hints
* visible progress through rounds
* reward loop like badges, streaks, discoveries, quest map

#### E. Light memory system

* remembers topics attempted
* surfaces rematches 2–5 days later
* adapts difficulty based on prior performance
* suggests adjacent curiosities

#### F. Voice-friendly experience

For MVP, this can be lightweight:

* speak questions aloud
* tap-to-answer or multiple choice
* optional parent-read format

Full natural spoken conversation can be deferred.

### MVP exclusions

* camera-based scavenger verification
* multiplayer remote family mode
* teacher dashboards
* school LMS integrations
* long-form curriculum paths
* full report cards
* community content marketplace

## 11. User Flows

### Flow 1: First-time parent

1. Parent downloads app.
2. Adds child profile.
3. Selects age, reading level, favorite topics.
4. Chooses family style: calm / balanced / energetic.
5. Sees suggested starter quests.
6. Launches first game in under 2 minutes.

### Flow 2: Topic to activity

1. Parent enters “Moon phases.”
2. Chooses “Car Trivia.”
3. Sets age 10, duration 8 minutes.
4. QuestKit generates 10 questions with hints and scorekeeping.
5. Parent starts voice mode.
6. App stores performance and suggests a follow-up challenge later.

### Flow 3: Chapter to game

1. Parent pastes a chapter excerpt.
2. Selects “Make this a game.”
3. QuestKit proposes three modes.
4. Parent previews and picks mini mystery.
5. Child plays.
6. App logs weak areas and later suggests a short callback challenge.

* accept topic strings
* accept pasted text excerpts up to defined length
* classify source type: book, science topic, comprehension, trivia topic

### Activity generation

* generate age-appropriate questions and challenges
* vary structure by mode
* include hints and answer explanations
* constrain outputs to safe, educational formats
* support replay/remix behavior

### Personalization

* store child profile
* infer topic familiarity over time
* adjust difficulty and pacing
* bias recommendations to family-preferred modes

### Progress and memory

* store topic history
* store performance signals
* identify weak vs strong concepts
* trigger spaced re-exposure through playful callbacks

### Safety and trust

* child-safe outputs
* no unsafe open-ended peer interaction
* parent visibility into what was generated
* stronger guardrails for factual correctness in educational content

## 14. Success Metrics

### North star metric

**Weekly successful learning play sessions per active family**

A successful session is one that is started and completed above a minimum engagement threshold.

### Core product metrics

* activity generation to launch conversion rate
* sessions per family per week
* average session completion rate
* day 7 / day 30 family retention
* percentage of sessions replayed or remixed
* number of saved topics per family
* repeat usage across 2+ modes

### Learning-effectiveness proxies

* improvement on repeated concepts over time
* hint dependency reduction over repeated sessions
* parent-rated educational usefulness
* child-rated fun score

### Trust metrics

* parent preview acceptance rate
* edit-before-launch rate
* content issue / bad generation rate
* factual correction rate

## 15. Differentiation

QuestKit should not compete head-on as a homework-answer product. That is crowded, low-trust, and easy to commoditize.

Instead, differentiation comes from:

* turning **any content into multiple playful formats**
* building for **family rituals and real-life moments**
* balancing **parent control with child delight**
* making **spaced repetition invisible**
* optimizing for **repeated use**, not one-off answers

In plain terms: most products help with school tasks. QuestKit helps families build a learning habit they actually want to repeat.

## 16. Risks

### Risk 1: Generic output

If activities feel templated or repetitive, retention dies.

**Mitigation:** mode-specific activity templates, novelty rotation, stronger personalization, high-quality generation constraints.

### Risk 2: Too educational for kids, too chaotic for parents

This is the central tension.

**Mitigation:** dual-mode UX, family-style settings, parent preview, tight tone controls.

### Risk 3: Weak trust due to factual errors

Educational products break fast when parents spot inaccuracies.

**Mitigation:** constrained domains in MVP, source-backed content generation where possible, QA layer for factual checks, explainable answer notes.

### Risk 4: High generation cost with weak retention

Consumer AI businesses die when engagement does not justify inference cost.

**Mitigation:** cache reusable quest structures, blend AI generation with templated gameplay engines, favor short sessions, reuse topic graphs.

### Risk 5: Looks like “just another AI app”

If the product feels like a generic prompt box, it loses defensibility.

**Mitigation:** build around purpose-specific interaction modes and strong opinionated workflows.

## 19. Technical/Product Strategy Notes

### Product architecture direction

Use a hybrid system:

* structured generation templates by mode
* adaptive personalization layer
* retrieval or source grounding for educational correctness where feasible
* lightweight progress model for each child/topic

### Why hybrid matters

Pure open-ended generation will be too expensive, too inconsistent, and too trust-fragile. The core product should be a **play engine with AI filling the content layer**, not AI improvising the entire experience every time.
